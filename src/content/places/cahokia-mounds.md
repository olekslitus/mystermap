Cahokia, in its eleventh-century prime, was larger than London. Its
Monks Mound — a 100-foot earthen pyramid still extant — covered fourteen
acres at the base, more than any pyramid at Giza. Around it, a planned
city of plazas, neighborhoods, and a wooden Stonehenge-like solar
calendar housed an estimated twenty thousand Mississippian people.

By 1400 CE the city was empty. The reasons remain debated: drought,
deforestation, internal political collapse, or some combination. The
people who survived dispersed; their descendants are among many tribes
across the Midwest and South.

The mounds remain. So does Mound 72, where archaeologists in 1967 found
a young man buried atop a beadwork falcon shaped from twenty thousand
shell beads, accompanied by what appear to have been four sacrificed
young men with their hands and heads removed, and a mass grave of
fifty-three sacrificed young women. The site is a UNESCO World Heritage
designation. It is also profoundly haunted in every sense the word can
hold.
