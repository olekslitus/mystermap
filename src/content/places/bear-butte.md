Bear Butte rises 1,253 feet above the prairie east of the Black Hills.
The Lakota call it Matȟó Pahá; the Cheyenne, Nóávose. It is a sacred
mountain to both peoples, and to many others who have visited it on
vision quests for at least four hundred documented years.

Crazy Horse was reportedly born nearby and fasted on the mountain as a
young man. The Cheyenne prophet Sweet Medicine received the Four Sacred
Arrows here. The trail to the summit passes a forest of trees draped in
prayer cloths and tobacco bundles — visitors are asked not to touch them.

Bear Butte is also a state park, fifty acres deeded back to the
Indigenous nations who hold the land sacred. The biker rallies of nearby
Sturgis press against its edges every August. The mountain remains.
