Mammoth Cave's 426 mapped miles make it the longest known cave system
on Earth, and surveyors find new passages every year. Indigenous people
explored its first chambers as much as 4,000 years ago — their cane
torches, gourd canteens, and human remains are still preserved in the
upper levels.

In the early nineteenth century, Mammoth Cave was a saltpeter mine,
worked largely by enslaved African Americans. Stephen Bishop, an
enslaved man who became Mammoth's most celebrated nineteenth-century
guide, mapped huge new sections in the 1840s and 1850s and was the
first person to cross what he named the Bottomless Pit. Bishop died in
1857 at the age of thirty-seven and is buried in Mammoth's Old Guide
Cemetery.

Visitors and current park rangers have reported: footsteps in unlit
passages, lanterns floating in the Bottomless Pit, the figure of a man
in a stovepipe hat (Bishop wore one) leading them back to the entrance
when they had become disoriented in the dark. The cave is dark in a
way that only caves are. The dark, the rangers say, is not empty.
