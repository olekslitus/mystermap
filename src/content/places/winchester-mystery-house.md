After her husband and infant daughter died, Sarah Winchester — heiress
to half the Winchester Repeating Arms Company fortune — moved west to a
rural California ranch in 1886. According to the legend that quickly
attached itself, a Boston medium told her that the ghosts of everyone
killed by a Winchester rifle were following her, and that the only way
to keep them confused was to never stop building.

For the next thirty-six years, until her death in 1922, Sarah did not
stop. The house at the end of South Winchester Boulevard grew to seven
stories, 160 rooms, with stairs that led to ceilings, doors that opened
onto two-story drops, and corridors that doubled back on themselves.
After the 1906 earthquake she sealed off thirty rooms entirely, ghosts
inside.

Whether Sarah herself believed the medium has never been clearly
established. She was a private and grieving woman, and the better
biographies suggest that her construction was an architectural
expression of grief, not theology. The house is a National Historic
Landmark. It is also one of the most reliably haunted addresses on
the West Coast — by Sarah, mostly, who long-time docents say still
walks the corridors after dark.
