In October 1974, Stephen King and his wife Tabitha checked into the
Stanley Hotel in Estes Park, Colorado on the last night of its season,
when the hotel was nearly empty. They were the only guests. King could
not sleep. He had a vivid nightmare about his three-year-old son being
chased through the hotel's hallways by a fire hose come alive, woke
up, and went out onto the balcony to smoke. By morning he had the bones
of *The Shining*.

The Overlook of the novel is, technically, a fiction. But every guest
who has stayed in Room 217 — King's room — knows what room they're in,
and asks the front desk about it. The hotel obliges. Its current
business model is roughly two-thirds normal hospitality, one-third
ghost tour. Both are profitable.

Reported phenomena include: piano music from the ballroom played by
F.O. Stanley, the long-dead founder; lights flickering in the closed
fourth-floor staff quarters; children laughing in Room 418. None of it
is provable. Most of it is, on reflection, exactly what a hotel like
this ought to have.
