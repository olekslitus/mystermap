On the night of August 21, 1955, eleven members of the Sutton family burst into the police station in Hopkinsville, Kentucky and reported being under siege at their farmhouse in Kelly. They had been firing shotguns and rifles for nearly four hours, they said, at small silver beings with large pointed ears, glowing yellow eyes, and arms that ended in claws. The figures climbed onto the roof, peered into the windows, and floated rather than ran.

The police found no occupants of the farmhouse but did find spent ammunition, holes in the screens, and frightened witnesses in numbers — eight adults and three children, all telling the same story. Project Blue Book opened a case file. The investigators concluded, eventually, that the Suttons had been spooked by a great horned owl. The Suttons disagreed.

The Kelly–Hopkinsville encounter became one of the most documented and baffling close-encounter cases of the twentieth century. It is the source, scholars now agree, of the term 'little green men.'

The town of Kelly holds a Little Green Men Festival each August. The Suttons are mostly gone. The story is still here.
