Denver International Airport opened in February 1995, sixteen months late
and roughly two billion dollars over budget. It sits on 53 square miles of
prairie twenty-five miles northeast of the city it serves — far enough
that the joke is, the airport has its own zip code, its own weather, and
(if certain corners of the internet are right) its own subterranean city.

The conspiracies attach themselves quickly. The murals by Leo Tanguma —
two large works showing a gas-masked soldier slaughtering a dove and
crying refugee children — are read as a coded blueprint for the New
World Order. The capstone marker near the south entrance is dedicated by
"The New World Airport Commission," an organization with no other public
existence. The runways, viewed from a low aerial angle, have been called
swastika-shaped. There is, indeed, a working network of automated
service tunnels beneath the terminals; whether they extend down five
levels to a Reptilian command bunker is a question the airport has not
satisfactorily answered.

And then there is Blucifer. The Blue Mustang sculpture by Luis Jiménez,
32 feet tall and weighing 9,000 pounds, with eyes that glow red after
dark, was installed at the airport's main entrance in 2008. Two years
before the installation, a portion of the unfinished sculpture fell on
Jiménez in his New Mexico studio and killed him. The horse was completed
by his sons. The eyes, lit from within, were Jiménez's tribute to his
father, a neon-sign maker. The locals do not find this comforting.

DIA itself, in recent years, has leaned into the lore — the airport's
own marketing now winks at gargoyles, lizard people, and tunnel
dwellers. The believers note, fairly, that this is exactly what a
conspiracy would do. The travelers, between flights, look at the murals
and find them genuinely difficult to explain.
