On the night of August 31, 1944, Aline Kearney of Mattoon, Illinois woke to a sweet, sickly smell drifting through her open bedroom window. Her legs went numb. She vomited. She could not stand for hours.

Within two weeks the Mattoon police had logged over a hundred similar reports — always at night, always in residential houses, always preceded by a sweet smell and followed by partial paralysis, nausea, and a burning sensation in the mouth. The local newspaper called the assailant the Mad Gasser. Vigilante patrols formed. The state police were called in. No one was caught.

The Mattoon gas attacks remain one of the most-studied mass-hysteria events in American social science. The competing theories — escaped chemical leak from the Atlas Diesel Engine works, prankster with chemistry-set access, panic seeded by wartime anxiety, real perpetrator never identified — have never fully resolved. Among Mattoon residents, the third explanation is the least popular.

The attacks ended as suddenly as they began. Mattoon kept the records. The Mad Gasser, by name, has not returned.
