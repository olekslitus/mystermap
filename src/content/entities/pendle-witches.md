In 1612, twelve people from the Pendle area of Lancashire were accused of murdering ten victims through witchcraft, making it one of the largest and best-documented witch trials in English history. The accusations began on 21 March when Alizon Device, a teenage girl from a poor local family, cursed pedlar John Law after he refused to sell her pins; Law suffered a stroke shortly after and blamed her powers. Nine of the accused were hanged at Gallows Hill in Lancaster on 20 August 1612, and their story was preserved by court clerk Thomas Potts in his 1613 book The Wonderfull Discoverie of Witches in the Countie of Lancaster, one of the most complete surviving records of any English witch trial.
