On a November night in 1971 in Bowie, Maryland, the Tannehill family came home to find their puppy missing and a story to tell: their teenage daughter and her friends had been chased on Fletchertown Road by a creature that walked upright on cloven hooves and carried an axe. By morning the puppy, named Ginger, was dead. The Washington Post ran the story.

The Goatman had, by then, already been a regional rumor for a decade. The earliest version named him as a USDA scientist at the Beltsville Agricultural Research Center whose attempt to splice human and goat DNA escaped the lab. The story migrated, with each retelling, from the laboratory to the woods.

Through the 1970s, Prince George's County teenagers reported being chased on Lottsford Road, Glenn Dale Road, and Fletchertown Road. The pattern is American: rural lover's lane, headlights low, something on hooves at the edge of the trees. The bridges in those woods still carry the rumor.

No physical evidence has ever been recovered. The Tannehills never found Ginger's body. The Goatman remains a Maryland-specific particular kind of fear — local, suburban, and very, very old in disguise.
