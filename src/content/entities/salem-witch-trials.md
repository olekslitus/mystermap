Between February 1692 and May 1693, more than two hundred people in and
around Salem Village (now Danvers), Massachusetts were accused of
witchcraft. Nineteen were hanged. One — Giles Corey — was pressed to
death under stones for refusing to enter a plea. Five more died in jail.
The accused were overwhelmingly women.

The trials remain the United States' most studied episode of mass
hysteria, theological certainty turned against neighbors. Salem itself
has spent three centuries reckoning with its own name, and in October
the town fills with witches of every other kind — the kind who do not
need to be hunted to walk down the street.
