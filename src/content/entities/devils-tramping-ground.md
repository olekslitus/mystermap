About fifty miles south-west of Raleigh, in the long-leaf pine of Chatham County, there is a circle of bare earth roughly forty feet across. Nothing has grown inside it for as long as anyone has kept records — not grass, not weeds, not pine seedlings. The locals call it the Devil's Tramping Ground.

The oldest written reference is John H. Wheeler's 'Reminiscences of North Carolina,' published 1882, which records the area's existing folklore: that the Devil walks there nightly in a tight circle, working out the world's affairs. An NC Department of Agriculture soil sample in 1957 found no chemical reason for the barrenness.

Visitors are told to leave nothing in the circle overnight. Sticks placed inside it are often found, by morning, thrown out. Coins disappear. Two centuries of campers report dogs that will not enter, and a hum at the base of the spine that they cannot quite name.

The site is on private land, but reachable. It is, deliberately, not promoted. The path in is overgrown. The circle, when you find it, is bare.
