The Pope Lick Trestle stands eighty feet above a narrow Kentucky creek east of Louisville. CSX freight trains use it daily. Climbing it is illegal, conspicuously dangerous, and — according to Louisville teenagers since at least the 1940s — guarded by a goat-headed man who lures the curious onto the tracks.

The legend's variants disagree on origin: a circus train derailment, a vengeful farmer, a butchered child returned. They agree on the warning. The Pope Lick Monster, the story goes, can imitate any voice, and once you hear your name called from the middle of the bridge you will not stop walking until the train hits you.

The trestle has killed several real people. A 19-year-old in 1988. A 26-year-old visitor from Ohio in 2016. A photographer the same year. Each death revives the legend in the local press, with a now-familiar weariness.

Louisville's parks department added a fence in 2016. People still climb.
