The Snallygaster is what Maryland gets when you mix German Palatine immigrants, the Appalachian foothills, and a slow century of newspaper editors with column inches to fill. The name is a corruption of 'Schneller Geist,' fast spirit, brought to the Monocacy Valley around 1735.

In 1909, the Snallygaster became a sensation. The Middletown Valley Register ran a series of stories describing a beast with one cyclopean eye, leathery wings, a metallic beak, and a taste for blood. Other papers picked the story up. President Theodore Roosevelt — apparently — considered organizing a hunt. The wave subsided.

In the early 1930s the creature returned, briefly, before being killed (the local press claimed) when it fell into a 2,500-gallon vat of moonshine and was destroyed by federal Prohibition agents. The Snallygaster has been quieter since.

The seven-pointed stars painted on Pennsylvania Dutch barns across western Maryland are sometimes said to have been hex-marks meant to keep the Snallygaster away. They still hold.
