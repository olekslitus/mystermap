The Benandanti were members of a nocturnal visionary cult in 16th-century Friuli who believed they left their bodies while asleep to fight malevolent witches (malandanti) on behalf of their community, wielding fennel stalks against the enemy's sorghum. Born with a caul, they saw themselves as defenders of the harvest and fertility — not servants of the devil. Their genuine folk tradition was gradually reframed by Inquisitors into a diabolical witch cult, until persecution extinguished it entirely by the 1630s. Historian Carlo Ginzburg brought them to wide academic attention in his 1966 book I Benandanti.
