La Lechuza is older than the United States. The legend traveled north from central Mexico with the people who carried it: a witch — usually a woman, usually wronged — who has bound her soul to an owl's body. By night she becomes the bird. By day she lives quietly in town.

The lechuza calls travelers by their full names. She mimics a baby's cry to lure mothers from their houses. To answer her is dangerous. To ignore her is harder.

In the Rio Grande Valley, the lechuza is the kind of folklore that survives because it works. Drivers along Highway 281 in Hidalgo County still recount close passes by an owl too large to be an owl. Texas Monthly's 2018 oral history collected dozens of accounts from Brownsville to Laredo. Most witnesses asked not to be named.

The defenses are old: an open Bible on the porch, a circle of salt at the threshold, the crucifix worn close to the throat. They work, the storytellers will tell you, because they always have.
